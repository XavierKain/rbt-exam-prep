What Is ABA?

Applied Behavior Analysis (ABA) is defined as the science in which tactics derived from the principles of behavior are applied systematically to improve socially significant behavior, and experimentation is used to identify the variables responsible for behavior change (Cooper, Heron, & Heward, 2020).

In simpler terms: ABA uses what we know about how learning works to help people develop useful skills and reduce behaviors that are harmful or interfere with learning.

The seven dimensions of ABA, as defined by Baer, Wolf, and Risley (1968), are:

  1. Applied ‚Äî Focuses on socially significant behaviors
  2. Behavioral ‚Äî Targets observable, measurable behaviors
  3. Analytic ‚Äî Demonstrates a functional relationship between intervention and behavior change
  4. Technological ‚Äî Procedures are described clearly enough to be replicated
  5. Conceptually Systematic ‚Äî Grounded in established behavioral principles
  6. Effective ‚Äî Produces practical results
  7. Generality ‚Äî Behavior changes last over time, across settings, and spread to other behaviors

Brief History of ABA

ABA has roots in the broader field of behaviorism, pioneered by researchers like B.F. Skinner, Ivan Pavlov, and John B. Watson:

  • 1938: B.F. Skinner publishes The Behavior of Organisms, establishing operant conditioning principles
  • 1960s: Researchers begin applying behavioral principles to help children with autism (Lovaas, 1987)
  • 1968: Baer, Wolf, and Risley publish the foundational paper defining applied behavior analysis in the Journal of Applied Behavior Analysis (JABA)
  • 1998: The Behavior Analyst Certification Board (BACB) is established to credential behavior analysts
  • 2014: The BACB introduces the Registered Behavior Technician (RBT) credential
  • Today: Over 120,000 RBTs and 60,000 BCBAs are certified worldwide

How Does ABA Therapy Work?

ABA therapy is based on the ABC model of behavior:

  • A ‚Äî Antecedent: What happens before the behavior (the trigger or context)
  • B ‚Äî Behavior: The observable action
  • C ‚Äî Consequence: What happens after the behavior (reinforcement or punishment)

By analyzing the antecedents and consequences of behavior, ABA therapists can:

  1. Increase desired behaviors (like communication, social skills, self-care) using reinforcement
  2. Decrease problematic behaviors (like aggression, self-injury, elopement) by modifying antecedents and consequences
  3. Teach new skills by breaking them into small, teachable steps
  4. Generalize skills across settings, people, and situations

Every ABA program begins with a comprehensive assessment conducted by a Board Certified Behavior Analyst (BCBA), who then develops an individualized treatment plan with measurable goals.

Common ABA Techniques

Discrete Trial Training (DTT)

A structured teaching method that breaks skills into small steps, presents them one at a time, and provides reinforcement for correct responses. Learn more in our complete DTT guide.

Natural Environment Teaching (NET)

Teaching skills in natural, everyday contexts rather than a structured setting. For example, teaching a child to request items during play.

Pivotal Response Training (PRT)

Targets "pivotal" areas of development (motivation, self-management, responsivity to cues) that produce broad improvements across many behaviors.

Functional Communication Training (FCT)

Teaches individuals to communicate their needs effectively, replacing problem behaviors that serve a communication function.

Task Analysis and Chaining

Breaking complex skills (like brushing teeth) into individual steps and teaching them in sequence (forward chaining, backward chaining, or total task presentation).

Token Economy

A reinforcement system where individuals earn tokens for desired behaviors, then exchange tokens for preferred items or activities.

Who Benefits from ABA?

ABA therapy is most commonly used for individuals with autism spectrum disorder, but its principles apply broadly:

  • Children with autism ‚Äî Communication, social skills, daily living skills, behavior reduction
  • Individuals with developmental disabilities ‚Äî Skill acquisition and independence
  • Children with ADHD ‚Äî Self-management and organizational skills
  • Traumatic brain injury patients ‚Äî Behavioral rehabilitation
  • Substance abuse ‚Äî Contingency management programs
  • Organizational settings ‚Äî Performance management, safety training (OBM)
  • Education ‚Äî Classroom management, precision teaching

What Does the Research Say?

ABA is considered an evidence-based best practice by multiple authoritative bodies:

  • The US Surgeon General (1999) identified ABA as the most effective intervention for autism
  • The American Psychological Association recognizes ABA as an evidence-based practice
  • The National Autism Center's National Standards Project (2015) classified ABA-based interventions as "established treatments"
  • Lovaas (1987) found that 47% of children receiving intensive ABA (40 hours/week) achieved normal intellectual and educational functioning
  • A meta-analysis by Viru√©s-Ortega (2010) found that long-term, comprehensive ABA programs produce medium to large positive effects on language, social functioning, and daily living skills

What to Expect in ABA Therapy

Initial Assessment

A BCBA conducts a comprehensive assessment using tools like the VB-MAPP (Verbal Behavior Milestones Assessment and Placement Program), ABLLS-R, or AFLS. This identifies the individual's current skill levels and areas for growth.

Treatment Plan

The BCBA creates an individualized treatment plan with specific, measurable goals. Plans are reviewed and updated regularly (typically every 6 months).

Therapy Sessions

An RBT implements the treatment plan during sessions, which may take place in the home, clinic, school, or community. Sessions typically last 2‚Äì4 hours and may occur 3‚Äì5 days per week.

Data Collection

RBTs collect data on every target behavior during every session. This data drives treatment decisions and ensures the therapy is producing measurable results.

Parent/Caregiver Training

Effective ABA programs include training for parents and caregivers so they can support skill development and behavior management outside of therapy sessions.

Addressing Criticisms of ABA

ABA therapy has faced criticism, particularly from some members of the autistic community. It's important to address these concerns honestly:

Historical concerns: Early ABA programs (1960s‚Äì1980s) sometimes used aversive procedures like punishment. Modern ABA, guided by the BACB Ethics Code for Behavior Analysts, emphasizes positive reinforcement, client assent, and dignity.

Concerns about "normalizing" behavior: Critics argue that some ABA goals focus on making autistic individuals "look normal" rather than supporting their well-being. Ethical, modern ABA programs prioritize functional skills that improve quality of life and respect neurodiversity.

Intensity: Some parents and advocates question whether 25‚Äì40 hours/week of therapy is appropriate for young children. Individual programs should be tailored to the child's needs, and research supports a range of intensities.

The best ABA practitioners prioritize the individual's autonomy, assent, and quality of life in all treatment decisions.

Frequently Asked Questions

What does ABA therapy stand for?

ABA stands for Applied Behavior Analysis. It is a scientific discipline that applies principles of learning and behavior to improve socially significant behaviors. ABA therapy is the most widely researched and evidence-based treatment for autism spectrum disorder (ASD).

Is ABA therapy only for autism?

No. While ABA therapy is most commonly associated with autism spectrum disorder, it is also used for ADHD, traumatic brain injury, substance abuse, dementia, organizational behavior management, and other conditions where behavior change is needed.

How many hours of ABA therapy per week is recommended?

Research suggests that comprehensive ABA programs of 25‚Äì40 hours per week produce the best outcomes for young children with autism. However, many children benefit from fewer hours (10‚Äì25/week), and the ideal amount depends on the individual's needs and goals.

Is ABA therapy covered by insurance?

Yes. All 50 US states have autism insurance mandates that require coverage of ABA therapy. Most private insurance plans, Medicaid, and TRICARE cover ABA therapy for individuals diagnosed with autism spectrum disorder.

Is ABA therapy harmful or controversial?

Modern ABA therapy focuses on positive reinforcement, skill building, and respecting the individual's autonomy. Some critics reference historical practices that used aversive methods, but contemporary ABA emphasizes ethical, person-centered approaches guided by the BACB Ethics Code.
