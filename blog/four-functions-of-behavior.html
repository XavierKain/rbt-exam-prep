The 4 Functions of Behavior in ABA (With Examples)

üìÖ February 2025 ‚è± 12 min read üìò ABA Fundamentals

One of the foundational principles of applied behavior analysis (ABA) is that all behavior serves a purpose. Understanding why a behavior occurs is essential for developing effective interventions. In ABA, behavior is maintained by one (or more) of four functions.

This concept is heavily tested on the RBT exam and is critical for daily practice as an RBT.

The 4 Functions Overview (SEAT)

A helpful mnemonic for remembering the four functions is SEAT:

LetterFunctionThe Behavior Gets the Person...
SSensory/AutomaticInternal stimulation (it feels good)
EEscape/AvoidanceRemoval of something unpleasant
AAttentionSocial interaction from others
TTangibleAccess to a desired item or activity

The first two are socially mediated (attention and tangible ‚Äî they require another person). Escape can be socially mediated (someone removes the demand) or non-socially mediated. Automatic reinforcement is non-socially mediated (the behavior itself is reinforcing, no other person is needed).

1. Attention (Social Reinforcement)

Definition: Behavior is maintained by receiving attention, interaction, or a reaction from another person.

Attention can take many forms:

  • Eye contact
  • Verbal reprimands ("Stop doing that!")
  • Comfort (hugs, reassurance)
  • Laughter
  • Physical contact
  • Peers looking at or talking about the behavior

Examples of Attention-Maintained Behavior

  • A child cries ‚Üí parent picks them up and comforts them ‚Üí crying increases when the child wants attention
  • A student makes jokes in class ‚Üí classmates laugh ‚Üí joking increases
  • A child hits their sibling ‚Üí parent scolds them ("Don't hit!") ‚Üí hitting continues because any attention is reinforcing
  • A toddler throws food ‚Üí parent reacts dramatically ‚Üí food throwing increases

Key insight: Attention doesn't have to be positive. Negative attention (scolding, reprimanding) can still reinforce behavior.

2. Escape/Avoidance

Definition: Behavior is maintained by the removal, postponement, or avoidance of an aversive task, demand, activity, or situation.

Examples of Escape-Maintained Behavior

  • A child screams during math ‚Üí teacher sends them to the hallway ‚Üí screaming increases during math (escape from work)
  • A student puts their head down ‚Üí teacher stops asking questions ‚Üí head-down behavior increases
  • A child has a tantrum when asked to clean up ‚Üí parent does the cleaning ‚Üí tantrums increase during clean-up time
  • An employee calls in sick on presentation days ‚Üí avoids presenting ‚Üí sick-day pattern increases

Key insight: Escape-maintained behavior is maintained by negative reinforcement ‚Äî the behavior removes something unpleasant.

3. Access to Tangibles/Activities

Definition: Behavior is maintained by obtaining a desired item, activity, or preferred event.

Examples of Tangible-Maintained Behavior

  • A child screams in the store ‚Üí parent buys them candy ‚Üí screaming in stores increases
  • A child grabs a toy from a peer ‚Üí obtains the toy ‚Üí grabbing increases
  • A teenager has a tantrum ‚Üí parent gives back their phone ‚Üí tantrums increase when phone is taken away
  • A child leads an adult by the hand to the cookie jar ‚Üí gets a cookie ‚Üí hand-leading increases

4. Automatic/Sensory Reinforcement

Definition: Behavior is maintained by the sensory stimulation it produces, independent of social consequences. The behavior itself is reinforcing.

Examples of Automatically-Maintained Behavior

  • A child rocks back and forth ‚Üí the vestibular stimulation feels good ‚Üí rocking continues
  • A person hums a song while alone ‚Üí the auditory feedback is pleasant ‚Üí humming continues
  • Hand-flapping ‚Üí proprioceptive/visual stimulation ‚Üí continues regardless of social context
  • Scratching an itch ‚Üí relief from discomfort ‚Üí scratching continues

Key insight: Automatic reinforcement is the only function that doesn't require another person. If a behavior occurs at similar rates when the person is alone vs. with others, it may be automatically reinforced.

Important note: Many self-stimulatory behaviors (stimming) are part of neurodivergent individuals' self-regulation. Modern ABA practice respects that many automatically-reinforced behaviors are not harmful and should not be targeted for reduction unless they pose a safety risk or significantly interfere with learning.

How to Identify the Function of Behavior

Identifying the function of a behavior is called functional behavior assessment (FBA). The BCBA leads this process, but RBTs play a crucial role in data collection.

Methods of Assessment

1. Indirect Assessment

  • Interviews with parents, teachers, caregivers
  • Rating scales (e.g., Motivation Assessment Scale ‚Äî MAS, Functional Assessment Screening Tool ‚Äî FAST)
  • Record reviews

2. Descriptive Assessment

  • ABC data collection: Recording Antecedents, Behaviors, and Consequences as they naturally occur
  • Scatterplot: Recording when behavior occurs across the day to identify patterns
  • Direct observation in natural settings

3. Functional Analysis (FA)

  • The gold standard for identifying behavior function
  • Involves systematically manipulating antecedents and consequences in controlled conditions
  • Typically includes: attention, escape, tangible, and alone/control conditions
  • Conducted by or under the close supervision of a BCBA

Quick Identification Guide

If the Behavior Tends to Occur When...Likely Function
Attention is diverted elsewhereAttention
A demand or task is presentedEscape
A preferred item is visible but unavailableTangible
The person is alone with nothing to doAutomatic/Sensory

Function-Based Treatment

Once the function is identified, the BCBA designs function-based interventions:

FunctionExample Interventions
AttentionPlanned ignoring (extinction) + frequent positive attention for appropriate behavior; teach appropriate ways to request attention
EscapeEscape extinction (follow through with demands) + teach appropriate break requests; modify task difficulty; use high-probability request sequences
TangibleDon't provide the item contingent on problem behavior + teach appropriate requesting (manding); use token economies
AutomaticProvide alternative sensory activities; environmental enrichment; response blocking (if behavior is dangerous); matched stimulation

The key principle: the intervention must match the function. An intervention designed for attention-maintained behavior won't work for escape-maintained behavior, and vice versa.

RBT Exam Tips

  • Memorize SEAT: Sensory, Escape, Attention, Tangible
  • Read scenarios carefully: Look for what happens before and after the behavior
  • Focus on the consequence: What does the person GET or AVOID as a result of the behavior?
  • Remember: all behavior has a function. Even behavior that seems random serves a purpose
  • Automatic = no social mediation: If the scenario shows behavior occurring when the person is alone, think automatic
  • Negative attention is still attention: Scolding, redirecting, or even saying "no" can reinforce attention-maintained behavior

Frequently Asked Questions

What are the 4 functions of behavior in ABA?

The 4 functions of behavior are: (1) Attention/Social ‚Äî behavior occurs to gain attention, (2) Escape/Avoidance ‚Äî behavior occurs to avoid a demand or situation, (3) Access to Tangibles/Activities ‚Äî behavior occurs to obtain a desired item, and (4) Automatic/Sensory ‚Äî behavior occurs because it is internally reinforcing. These are remembered using the acronym SEAT.

What does SEAT stand for in ABA?

SEAT is a mnemonic for the 4 functions: S = Sensory/Automatic, E = Escape, A = Attention, T = Tangible. All behavior serves at least one of these four functions.

How do you determine the function of a behavior?

The function is determined through functional behavior assessment (FBA), which includes: indirect methods (interviews, questionnaires), descriptive assessment (ABC data collection, direct observation), and functional analysis (systematic manipulation of conditions).

Can a behavior have more than one function?

Yes. A single behavior can serve multiple functions depending on the context. For example, a child may tantrum for attention in one situation and to escape a demand in another.
