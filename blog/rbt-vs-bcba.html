RBT vs BCBA: Salary, Requirements & Career Path

If you're considering a career in Applied Behavior Analysis (ABA), you've probably seen two certifications come up: RBT and BCBA. They're both part of the same field, but they're very different in terms of education, responsibilities, and earning potential.

This guide breaks down everything you need to know to decide which path is right for you ‚Äî or how to use one as a stepping stone to the other.

What Does an RBT Do?

RBTs are the front-line providers of ABA therapy. They work directly with clients (often children with autism) to implement behavior intervention plans designed by a BCBA.

Day-to-day responsibilities include:

  • Running therapy sessions (DTT, NET, play-based learning)
  • Collecting data on behavior and skill acquisition
  • Implementing behavior reduction strategies
  • Communicating with the supervising BCBA
  • Working with families to carry over skills at home

What Does a BCBA Do?

BCBAs are the supervisors and program designers. They assess clients, create individualized treatment plans, train RBTs, and oversee the entire therapeutic process.

Day-to-day responsibilities include:

  • Conducting assessments (FBAs, VB-MAPP, ABLLS)
  • Writing behavior intervention plans
  • Supervising and training RBTs
  • Analyzing data and adjusting programs
  • Meeting with families, schools, and other professionals
  • Managing caseloads

How to Become an RBT

  1. Have a high school diploma (or equivalent)
  2. Complete a 40-hour training program ‚Äî available online
  3. Pass a background check
  4. Complete a competency assessment ‚Äî observed by a BCBA
  5. Pass the RBT exam ‚Äî 85 questions, $50 fee

Total timeline: 4 to 8 weeks from start to certified.

How to Become a BCBA

  1. Earn a bachelor's degree (any field)
  2. Complete a master's degree in behavior analysis, psychology, or education with BACB-approved coursework
  3. Accumulate 1,500-2,000 supervised fieldwork hours
  4. Pass the BCBA exam ‚Äî 185 questions, 4 hours

Total timeline: 2 to 4 years after bachelor's degree.

The Career Path: RBT ‚Üí BCaBA ‚Üí BCBA

Many successful BCBAs started as RBTs. Here's the typical career progression:

  1. RBT ‚Äî Get certified, start working, gain hands-on experience
  2. BCaBA (optional) ‚Äî Board Certified Assistant Behavior Analyst, requires a bachelor's degree
  3. BCBA ‚Äî Full behavior analyst, requires a master's degree
  4. BCBA-D (optional) ‚Äî Doctoral-level board certification

Starting as an RBT gives you invaluable clinical experience that will make you a better BCBA. Many graduate programs prefer applicants with RBT experience, and some employers offer tuition assistance for RBTs pursuing their master's degree.

Job Outlook

Both RBT and BCBA roles are in high demand. The Bureau of Labor Statistics projects the ABA field to grow significantly due to:

  • Increasing autism diagnoses
  • Insurance mandates for ABA coverage in most states
  • Growing awareness of ABA effectiveness
  • Expansion into new populations (TBI, substance abuse, OBM)
üí° Pro Tip: RBT positions rarely stay vacant long. If you're looking for a career change with low barrier to entry and immediate job prospects, RBT is one of the fastest paths. You can be working within 2 months of starting your training.

Which Should You Choose?

Choose RBT if:

  • You want to start working in ABA quickly
  • You enjoy hands-on work with clients
  • You want to "test the waters" before committing to a master's degree
  • You're looking for a career change with a low barrier to entry

Choose BCBA (or start planning for it) if:

  • You want to design programs and lead a team
  • Higher earning potential is important to you
  • You're interested in research and clinical decision-making
  • You want more professional autonomy

Best strategy: Start as an RBT now, gain experience, and decide later if you want to pursue BCBA.
